Create information theory chapter as a sequence of extremely simple exercises.
Add examples of inputs and outputs.

1. You have data containing As and Bs. You can only transmit 0s and 1s.
   Come up with encoder and decoder strategies such that there is no confusion.

2. Now, you have data containing A, B and Cs. You can only transmit 0s and 1s.
   Come up with encoding and decoding strategies such that there is no confusion.
   If we decode an encoded sequence of 0s and 1s, we should be able to get only
   the original without ambiguity. (Key concept: prefix-free codes.)

3. Now, in the previous problem, what if we have more than 50% A, more than 25% B,
   20% C and 5% D. Try various strategies and various examples and find the length
   of encoded data.

4. Try to figure out a strategy that can give you the shortest length.
   (Lead to Huffman coding: greedy merge of two rarest symbols.)

5. Now, write a generalized encode(data, frequencies) that returns encoded_data and
   scheme. And write a generalized decode(encoded_data, scheme).

Pedagogical arc:
- Part 1: A/B -> 1-bit fixed codes (trivially prefix-free)
- Part 2: A/B/C -> need >=2 bits; fixed-width vs variable-length prefix-free;
          introduce is_prefix_free(); show variable scheme saves bits
- Part 3: Four-symbol frequencies -> avg_bits(); compare 3 schemes
- Part 4: Huffman tree (build_huffman_tree, assign_codes, huffman_scheme);
          compare to Shannon entropy
- Part 5: generalized encode(data, frequencies) and decode(encoded_data, scheme)
